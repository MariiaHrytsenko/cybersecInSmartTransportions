Cyber Threats to Transport Companies

False Data Injection
Source: ResearchGate
Attackers inject false information into the V2X network, tricking vehicles into reacting to non-existent obstacles. This can lead to unnecessary braking, detours, or even accidents.

Consequences: Traffic disruption, increased risk of collisions, reduced trust in autonomous systems.
Mitigation: Cross-validate data from multiple sources (e.g. sensors, cloud), implement anomaly detection algorithms, apply cryptographic integrity checks to transmitted data.
Sybil Attack
Source: ResearchGate
An attacker generates multiple fake vehicle identities within a V2X system. This creates a false sense of traffic congestion, manipulating real traffic flows and rerouting.

Consequences: Traffic inefficiencies, fuel waste, increased travel time, network overload.
Mitigation: Use identity authentication via PKI (Public Key Infrastructure), monitor for unrealistic behavior patterns, apply radio fingerprinting or hardware-based ID verification.
QR Code Phishing (“Quishing”) – A Rising Global Cyber Threat
Source: Perception Point
QR code phishing attacks—also known as “quishing”—have surged globally. These malicious QR codes appear identical to legitimate ones and are often placed in public or sent via email. Scanning them can redirect users to phishing sites that steal credentials or install malware.

Consequences: Credential theft, unauthorized access to corporate systems, financial loss, malware infection.
Mitigation: Educate users to avoid scanning unknown QR codes, use mobile security apps with QR scanning protection, block image-based attachments in corporate emails.
GPS Spoofing – Scandinavia & Baltics (2023)
Source: The Baltic Times
In 2023, GPS spoofing incidents disrupted aircraft and maritime navigation across Scandinavia and the Baltics. Fake GPS signals misled onboard systems, causing incorrect positioning.

Consequences: Route deviation, navigational errors, possible accidents in air or sea traffic, loss of service availability.
Mitigation: Use GNSS signal authentication (e.g., Galileo OSNMA), fuse GPS with inertial navigation systems (INS), detect anomalies via signal strength and timing analysis.
